STRIDE Methodology

The STRIDE methodology, developed by Microsoft, identifies threats across six categories: Spoofing (violates Authentication), Tampering (Integrity), Repudiation (Non-Repudiation), Information Disclosure (Confidentiality), Denial of Service (Availability), and Elevation of Privilege (Authorization)

  • • Spoofing - Pretending to be someone else
  • • Tampering - Unauthorized data modification
  • • Repudiation - Denial of actions without audit trails
  • • Information Disclosure - Exposing data to unauthorized parties
  • • Denial of Service - Overloading resources to disrupt service
  • • Elevation of Privilege - Gaining unauthorized permissions

Four Question Framework

The Four Question Framework in threat modeling consists of: (1) What are we working on? (2) What can go wrong? (3) What are we going to do about it? (4) Did we do a good job? This framework helps organize the threat modeling process across different levels of abstraction and system lifecycles.

PASTA Framework

PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis) is a risk-focused, seven-stage threat modeling methodology that emphasizes business context and prioritizes high-impact threats. The stages are: 1) Identify assets and define application architecture, 2) Define threat environment, 3) Decompose application functionally and analyze exploitable weaknesses, 4) Identify key attack scenarios, 5) Conduct structured analysis using STRIDE, 6) Identify threat agents, and 7) Prioritize and mitigate threats.

DREAD Scoring

DREAD is a Microsoft-developed threat scoring framework that assesses risks based on five components: Damage potential, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected users, and Discoverability; each threat is rated from 1 to 10 per component, and the total score determines priority for mitigation.

CVSS System

Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), standardized by NIST, evaluates vulnerabilities using a severity scale up to 10; the process includes identifying vulnerabilities, gathering information, assigning base scores, optionally determining environmental scores, prioritizing, mitigating risks, and continuous monitoring.

Video-Surveillance-as-a-Service (VSaaS)

VSaaS is a cloud-based subscription model that eliminates upfront hardware investments, enables scalability, remote accessibility, and secure data transmission via encryption. Platforms like Arcules offer centralized management of live/recorded video, alarms, and analytics across distributed locations through a hybrid approach combining cloud flexibility with on-premises reliability.

  • • Eliminates upfront hardware investments

  • • Provides secure data transmission via encryption
  • • Offers centralized management capabilities

AI-Powered Video Analytics

AI-powered video surveillance enables predictive security, facial recognition for access control and tracking, Automatic License Plate Recognition (ALPR) for real-time vehicle identification and watchlist cross-referencing, object detection (e.g., weapons, unattended bags), and automation of monitoring tasks.

  • • Predictive security capabilities

  • • Automatic License Plate Recognition

IoT Integration

Technology and Applications

Facial recognition technology (FRT) identifies individuals by analyzing facial features from images or videos, with applications in law enforcement, border control, access control, mobile authentication, and targeted advertising. Advancements in deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have significantly improved accuracy, often surpassing human performance on benchmark datasets as of 2018.

Key Applications:

  • • Law enforcement and suspect identification
  • • Border control and identity verification
  • • Access control for secure facilities
  • • Mobile phone authentication
  • • Targeted advertising systems

Privacy and Regulatory Concerns

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), effective since 2018, governs personal data processing, including biometric data such as facial images. Article 9 classifies biometric data as special category data, prohibiting its use for identification without explicit consent or legal exemptions. The proposed Commercial Facial Recognition Privacy Act of 2019 would prohibit commercial entities from using FRT for identification or tracking without affirmative user consent.

Regulatory Frameworks:

  • • GDPR in the European Union
  • • Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act
  • • California Consumer Privacy Act
  • • Proposed federal legislation

Clearview AI Case Study

Clearview AI, founded in 2016 and based in New York, scraped over three billion facial images from social media and public websites to build a biometric database used by more than 600 U.S. law enforcement agencies including the FBI and Department of Homeland Security. The company faced legal challenges in Illinois, Vermont, New York, and Virginia, and received cease-and-desist letters from Twitter and Google over unauthorized data collection practices.

Legal Challenges:

  • • Violations of BIPA in Illinois
  • • Canadian privacy law violations
  • • Cease-and-desist from social platforms

Regulatory Actions:

  • • Swedish police fined 2.5 million SEK
  • • Canadian privacy authority findings
  • • Municipal bans in US cities

System Architecture and Implementation

Predictive policing involves using machine-learning models on historical crime data and additional inputs such as social media content, CCTV footage, biometrics, demographic indicators, socio-economic metrics, weather, and traffic patterns to forecast when and where crimes are likely to occur; it falls into two categories: location-based (predicting high-risk areas/times) and person-based (identifying individuals or groups more likely to commit or be victims of crime).

Key Components:

  • • Historical crime data analysis
  • • Real-time data integration
  • • Machine learning algorithms
  • • Predictive modeling systems

Ethical Concerns and Bias

Algorithmic bias arises because predictive models rely on historical crime data shaped by systemic biases and discriminatory policing; this leads to over-policing in minority neighborhoods, reinforcing existing inequalities and generating feedback loops where increased police presence yields more recorded crime, further skewing future predictions.

Bias Issues:

  • • Historical data bias
  • • Feedback loops
  • • Disproportionate targeting
  • • Racial profiling concerns

Case Studies and Real-World Applications

Chicago's Strategic Subject List

Chicago's Strategic Subject List (SSL) combines crime data with social network analysis to identify people at high risk for involvement in gun violence; a study found mixed results on its effectiveness in reducing gun violence, raising concerns about fairness and equity despite some evidence of crime reduction.

Los Angeles Strategic Extraction and Restoration

The LASER program identified 124 individuals as potential repeat offenders, of whom 87 were arrested at least once for similar crimes within the subsequent period, indicating short-term operational effectiveness but raising concerns about targeting criteria.

International Examples

Japan has used intelligent police tools based on deep learning since before the 2020 Tokyo Olympic Games; the Crime Nabi system, developed by a private start-up, is claimed to be over 50 percent more effective than conventional policing tactics in identifying high-risk areas.

IoT Device Identification Systems

The US11115799B1 patent describes a system for IoT device discovery and identification that uses network traffic analysis to classify devices. The system employs a hybrid approach combining rule-based methods with machine learning techniques, including supervised and unsupervised learning models such as neural networks (multi-layer perceptron with convolutional neural network) and extreme gradient boosting (XGB).

Technical Components:

  • • Network traffic analysis
  • • Machine learning classification
  • • Pattern ID generation
  • • Passive AAA functionality

Anomaly Detection Systems

IoT-PRIDS is a host-based anomaly detection system for IoT networks that leverages 'packet representations'—a fixed-size vector summarizing key header fields, communication patterns, and service usage—to create baseline profiles of normal device behavior using only benign traffic. It maps packets to representations by extracting and engineering features from Layer 2–5 headers.

Detection Capabilities:

  • • Web attack detection
  • • Reconnaissance detection
  • • DoS/DDoS protection
  • • High efficiency performance

Environmental Monitoring Applications

IoT-based environmental pollution monitoring systems integrate sensing, communication, and real-time data processing in environmental surveillance. These systems follow a four-layer model: (1) Perception layer uses sensors to collect data, (2) Transmission layer employs protocols like Zigbee, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, (3) Computation layer processes data using platforms like Arduino, Raspberry Pi, and (4) Application layer delivers real-time monitoring interfaces.

Sensor Technologies:

  • • Air quality sensors
  • • Water quality monitors
  • • Soil contamination detectors
  • • Noise pollution sensors

Communication Protocols:

  • • Zigbee for low power
  • • LoRaWAN for long range
  • • Wi-Fi for high bandwidth
  • • Bluetooth for proximity

Privacy Regulations

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) classifies biometric data as a special category of personal data, requiring explicit consent for processing and granting individuals the right to object, providing stronger protections than many U.S. frameworks. The Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA), enacted in 2008, regulates private entities by requiring informed written consent before collecting biometric data, prohibiting sale or profit from such data, and mandating protection equivalent to confidential information.

Key Regulations:

  • • GDPR - EU data protection
  • • BIPA - Illinois biometric privacy
  • • CCPA - California consumer privacy
  • • EU AI Act - Artificial intelligence

Patent and Innovation Framework

There were 10 times as many AI patent applications published worldwide in 2019 as in 2013, with the majority of these applications still awaiting examination as of the report's publication. From 2009 to 2019, the Chinese patent office saw a 500 percent increase in AI patent applications, 90 percent of which were domestic; during the same period, the U.S. patent office experienced a 35 percent increase in AI patent applications, with 48 percent being domestic filings.

Innovation Trends:

  • • Exponential patent growth
  • • Geographic distribution
  • • Technology specialization
  • • Legal framework evolution

International Standards and Compliance

NIST Frameworks

NIST collaborates with standards-developing organizations by providing expert input; for example, after investigating structural failures like the World Trade Center and Champlain Towers South, NIST provides safety recommendations that groups such as the International Code Council may translate into building codes and standards.

ISO Standards

International standards bodies include the International Organization for Standardization (ISO), the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), and ASTM International, all of which collaborate globally with public and private entities to develop standards for surveillance and security technologies.

Compliance Requirements

Regulatory compliance for surveillance systems includes data protection, privacy impact assessments, transparency requirements, and accountability mechanisms. Organizations must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure compliance with applicable laws and regulations.
