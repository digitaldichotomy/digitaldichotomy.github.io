The Algorithm's Core Assumption

Every engagement algorithm operates on one fundamental assumption: engagement leads to more engagement. Click, like, comment, share—each action signals to the algorithm that it's doing its job correctly. The 15-Second Dwell breaks this assumption completely.

The Logic Bomb

The 15-Second Dwell creates a logical contradiction: high initial interest (click) followed by zero subsequent engagement (immediate exit). This pattern breaks the algorithm's predictive model, forcing it to expend massive computational resources trying to understand what went "wrong."

The 15-Second Dwell Protocol

This is a surgical strike against algorithmic control. It's not about avoiding content—it's about engaging in a way that poisons the training data and wastes computational resources.

1

Identify Target Content

Look for manipulative content designed to provoke strong emotional responses: outrage bait, anxiety-inducing headlines, divisive political content, or algorithmically amplified "trending" topics.

Target indicators: Clickbait headlines, emotionally charged language, us-vs-them framing, urgency triggers.

2

Engage for Exactly 15 Seconds

Click on the content and remain on the page for precisely 15 seconds. Scroll slightly, maybe hover over elements, but do not click anything else. The goal is to simulate genuine interest.

Why 15 seconds: This duration is long enough to register as meaningful engagement but short enough to make the immediate exit seem suspicious.

3

Terminate All Engagement

At exactly 15 seconds, close the tab completely. Do not go back to the platform. Do not engage with any other content. The sudden disengagement is crucial.

Algorithmic impact: The system registers high engagement value followed by zero conversion, creating a data anomaly that forces model retraining.
